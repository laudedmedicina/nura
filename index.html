Heart Failure
HFrEF / HFpEFICD-10: I50High Mortality
Clinical pearl: BNP > 400 pg/mL or NT-proBNP > 900 pg/mL strongly supports acute decompensated HF. Always assess ejection fraction — management differs significantly between HFrEF (EF < 40%) and HFpEF (EF ≥ 50%).
Diagnostic Criteria
Framingham: 2 major or 1 major + 2 minor required for diagnosis
Echo: Assess EF, wall motion, valvular disease, diastolic dysfunction (E/e' ratio)
Labs: BNP/NT-proBNP, BMP, LFTs, CBC, TSH, iron studies
CXR: Cardiomegaly, Kerley B lines, bilateral pleural effusions, pulmonary edema
ECG: LVH, LBBB — triggers CRT consideration if EF < 35%
First-Line Drugs — HFrEF
Sacubitril / Valsartan
ARNI — Cornerstone therapy
Start: 24/26 mg BID → target 97/103 mg BID. Replace ACEi/ARB.
Reduces mortality by 20% vs enalapril (PARADIGM-HF). Washout 36h from ACEi before starting.
Carvedilol / Metoprolol succinate
Beta-blocker
Carvedilol: 3.125 mg BID → 25–50 mg BID. Start only when euvolemic.
Never initiate in acute decompensation. Titrate slowly over weeks.
Empagliflozin / Dapagliflozin
SGLT2 Inhibitor — "Fourth Pillar"
Empagliflozin 10 mg OD. Benefit regardless of DM status.
Reduces HF hospitalization and CV death (EMPEROR-Reduced).
Spironolactone / Eplerenone
MRA
Spironolactone 25 mg OD. Monitor K⁺ and creatinine closely.
Avoid if K⁺ > 5.0 or eGFR < 30. Eplerenone preferred post-MI.
Atrial Fibrillation
Paroxysmal / Persistent / PermanentICD-10: I48
Rule of thumb: Always calculate CHA₂DS₂-VASc before anticoagulation. Score ≥ 2 (males) or ≥ 3 (females) → anticoagulate.
Diagnostic Tips
ECG: Irregularly irregular rhythm, absent P waves, fibrillatory baseline
Classify: Paroxysmal (< 7 days), persistent (> 7 days), long-standing (> 1 year), permanent
TTE: LV function, valvular disease, LA size (> 45 mm → less likely cardioversion success)
Labs: TSH (thyrotoxicosis!), electrolytes, renal function, CBC
Pharmacotherapy
Apixaban
DOAC — Anticoagulation (preferred)
5 mg BID (2.5 mg BID if ≥ 2 of: age ≥ 80, weight ≤ 60 kg, Cr ≥ 1.5)
ARISTOTLE: Superior to warfarin in stroke prevention and major bleeding.
Bisoprolol / Metoprolol
Beta-blocker — Rate control
Bisoprolol 2.5–10 mg OD. Target HR < 80 bpm at rest.
Amiodarone
Class III — Rhythm control
Loading: 200 mg TID × 4 wks → 200 mg OD maintenance
Most effective antiarrhythmic but significant toxicity. Regular monitoring essential.
Acute Coronary Syndrome
STEMI / NSTEMI / UAICD-10: I21–I24
STEMI rule: Door-to-balloon time < 90 min. Aspirin 300 mg + Ticagrelor 180 mg loading before transfer.
Diagnostic Tips
STEMI ECG: ≥ 1 mm ST elevation in ≥ 2 contiguous limb leads or ≥ 2 mm precordial, new LBBB
Troponin: High-sensitivity — measure at 0h and 1–3h. Rise and/or fall pattern is diagnostic
Posterior MI: ST depression V1–V3 with dominant R → do posterior leads (V7–V9)
RV MI: Inferior STEMI + hypotension → right-sided leads (V3R–V4R)
Dual Antiplatelet + Therapy
Aspirin + Ticagrelor
DAPT — Standard of care
Aspirin 300 mg load → 75–100 mg OD. Ticagrelor 180 mg load → 90 mg BID
Continue DAPT for 12 months post-ACS.
Enoxaparin / UFH
Anticoagulation — Acute phase
Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg SC BID (adjust for eGFR). UFH if PCI imminent.
High-intensity Statin
HMG-CoA Reductase Inhibitor
Atorvastatin 80 mg OD or Rosuvastatin 40 mg OD. Start within 24h.
Target LDL < 1.4 mmol/L. Add ezetimibe if not at goal.
Hypertension
Essential / SecondaryICD-10: I10
Hypertensive emergency: BP > 180/120 with end-organ damage → reduce MAP by ≤ 25% in first hour with IV agents. Do NOT normalize acutely.
Diagnostic Tips
Confirm: ABPM preferred — diagnoses white-coat and masked HTN
Secondary causes: Young patients, resistant HTN — screen CKD, primary aldosteronism, renovascular, OSA, pheo
End-organ damage: Fundoscopy, ECG (LVH), urinalysis (proteinuria), eGFR, ABI

5–10 mg OD. Preferred in elderly and Black patients.
Side effect: peripheral edema.

ACE Inhibitor
Ramipril 2.5–10 mg OD. Monitor K⁺ and creatinine. Avoid in pregnancy.
Indapamide
Thiazide-like diuretic
1.5 mg SR OD. Fewer metabolic effects than hydrochlorothiazide.
Spironolactone
MRA — Resistant HTN (4th line)
25–50 mg OD. Most effective add-on. Monitor K⁺ closely.
Asthma
Intermittent / PersistentICD-10: J45
Acute severe warning: PEFR < 50%, silent chest, cyanosis → nebulized salbutamol + IV magnesium sulfate + systemic corticosteroids. Call ICU early.
Diagnostic Tips
Spirometry: FEV₁/FVC < 0.7 with ≥ 12% reversibility after bronchodilator confirms diagnosis
FeNO: > 50 ppb suggests eosinophilic inflammation — guides steroid response
Allergy workup: Skin prick test or specific IgE for uncontrolled/severe asthma
Step Therapy (GINA 2024)
Budesonide/Formoterol
ICS/LABA — MART regimen (preferred)
100/6 µg as reliever AND maintenance (SMART therapy).

Dupilumab / Mepolizumab
Biologic — Severe eosinophilic asthma
Dupilumab 200–300 mg SC q2wks. Mepolizumab 100 mg SC monthly.
Consider when blood eos ≥ 300/µL or FeNO ≥ 25 ppb with uncontrolled severe asthma.
COPD
GOLD Stage I–IVICD-10: J44
Acute exacerbation: Prednisolone 40 mg/day × 5 days. Antibiotics if purulent sputum. Target SpO₂ 88–92% — avoid over-oxygenation.
Diagnostic Tips
Spirometry: Post-bronchodilator FEV₁/FVC < 0.7 confirms airflow limitation
ABG: Chronic hypercapnia (PaCO₂ > 6 kPa) → assess for home NIV candidacy
GOLD ABE assessment: Classify by FEV₁ + symptom burden (mMRC/CAT) + exacerbation history
Pharmacotherapy (GOLD 2024)
Tiotropium (Spiriva)
LAMA — Cornerstone therapy
18 µg OD via HandiHaler or 2.5 µg via Respimat.

100/62.5/25 µg OD. For frequent exacerbators with blood eos ≥ 300/µL.
IMPACT trial: significant reduction in exacerbations vs dual therapy.

Typical / AtypicalICD-10: J18
Severity: CURB-65 guides site-of-care. Score ≥ 3 → hospital. Score ≥ 4 → consider ICU.
Diagnostic Tips
CXR: Lobar consolidation (typical: S. pneumoniae) vs diffuse interstitial (atypical: Mycoplasma, Legionella)
Legionella: Urine antigen — suspect in severe CAP, hyponatremia, GI symptoms, smokers
PCT: > 0.25 µg/L supports bacterial infection — guides antibiotic duration
Antibiotic Therapy
Amoxicillin + Clarithromycin
Mild-moderate CAP
Amoxicillin 1 g TID + Clarithromycin 500 mg BID × 5–7 days PO
Pip-Tazo + Azithromycin
Severe CAP / ICU

Type 2 Diabetes
T2DMICD-10: E11
2024 ADA approach: In patients with CVD, HF, or CKD: SGLT2i or GLP-1 RA are first-line regardless of HbA1c.
Diagnostic Tips
Diagnosis: HbA1c ≥ 6.5%, FPG ≥ 7 mmol/L, 2h PG ≥ 11.1 (OGTT)
Complications screening: Annual urine ACR, eGFR, retinal exam, monofilament foot, lipids, BP
HbA1c targets: < 7% most patients; < 6.5% young/no CVD; < 8% elderly/frail
Evidence-Based Pharmacotherapy
Metformin
Biguanide — Foundation therapy
500 mg OD → target 1000 mg BID. Hold if eGFR < 30.
Semaglutide (Ozempic)
GLP-1 RA — Preferred add-on
0.25 mg SC weekly → 0.5 mg → 1 mg. Oral: 3 mg OD escalate monthly.
SUSTAIN-6: 26% ↓ MACE. First-line with ASCVD.
Empagliflozin (Jardiance)
SGLT2 Inhibitor
10–25 mg OD before first meal. Avoid if eGFR < 20.
EMPA-REG: 38% ↓ CV death. Preferred in HFrEF and CKD.
Hypothyroidism
Primary / CentralICD-10: E03
Myxedema coma: Hypothermia, altered consciousness, bradycardia → IV levothyroxine 200–400 µg loading + liothyronine + IV hydrocortisone.
Diagnostic Tips
TSH: Best screening. Elevated TSH + low fT4 = primary. Normal/low TSH + low fT4 = central
Hashimoto's: TPO antibodies — most common cause in iodine-sufficient areas
Pregnancy: TSH targets — T1: < 2.5, T2–T3: < 3.0
Pharmacotherapy
Levothyroxine (T4)
Thyroid hormone — First-line
1.6 µg/kg/day. Start low (25–50 µg) in elderly/CAD. Increase by 25 µg every 4–6 wks.
Take fasting, 30–60 min before breakfast. Avoid concurrent iron, calcium, PPIs.
Liver Cirrhosis
Compensated / DecompensatedICD-10: K74
Variceal prophylaxis: All cirrhosis → endoscopy at diagnosis. Large varices → NSBB (propranolol/carvedilol) or band ligation.
Diagnostic Tips
FibroScan: LSM > 12.5 kPa suggests cirrhosis. Non-invasive.
Labs: Thrombocytopenia + raised INR + hypoalbuminaemia = portal hypertension
MELD score: > 15 → transplant evaluation. > 25 → high urgency
Pharmacotherapy by Complication
Spironolactone + Furosemide
Diuretics — Ascites
Spiro 100 mg : Furo 40 mg ratio. Max weight loss: 500 g/day.
Restrict Na < 90 mmol/day. Stop if Na⁺ < 125 (HRS risk).
Rifaximin + Lactulose
Hepatic encephalopathy prevention
Rifaximin 550 mg BID + Lactulose 15–30 mL BID–TID. Target 2–3 soft stools/day.
GERD / Reflux Disease
NERD / ERD / Barrett'sICD-10: K21
Alarm features: Dysphagia, weight loss, haematemesis, age > 55 with new dyspepsia → urgent endoscopy.
Diagnostic Tips
Empirical PPI trial: 4–8 weeks is diagnostic and therapeutic for typical symptoms
24h pH-impedance: Gold standard for reflux quantification — useful in PPI non-responders
Pharmacotherapy
Omeprazole / Lansoprazole
PPI — First-line
Omeprazole 20–40 mg OD, 30–60 min before breakfast. 4–8 week course.
Long-term risks: hypomagnesemia, B12 deficiency, C. diff, fractures.
Vonoprazan
P-CAB (Potassium-competitive acid blocker)
10–20 mg OD. Faster onset, not affected by CYP2C19 polymorphism.

Diagnostic Tips
Dipstick: Nitrites + leucocyte esterase — high NPV if both negative
Do not treat ASB: Only treat in pregnancy and pre-urological procedures

Nitrofurantoin
Uncomplicated lower UTI — First-line

Ciprofloxacin
Complicated UTI / Pyelonephritis
500 mg BID × 7–10 days (pyelonephritis).
Ceftriaxone / Pip-Tazo
Urosepsis — IV therapy
Ceftriaxone 2 g IV OD or Pip-Tazo 4.5 g IV TDS. De-escalate per sensitivities.
Chronic Kidney Disease
G1–G5 / A1–A3ICD-10: N18
KDIGO 2024: SGLT2 inhibitors recommended for all CKD (eGFR ≥ 20) regardless of diabetes — reduce progression and CV death.
Diagnostic Tips
CKD staging: CKD-EPI for eGFR. Combine GFR category (G1–G5) with albuminuria (A1–A3)
Complications: CKD-MBD (PTH, Ca²⁺, phosphate, vitamin D), anaemia, hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis

Dapagliflozin (Farxiga)
SGLT2 Inhibitor — Nephroprotective
10 mg OD. Use from eGFR ≥ 20. Continue until dialysis once started.

Finerenone (Kerendia)
Non-steroidal MRA
10–20 mg OD (dose by eGFR). Monitor K⁺ at 4 weeks. Avoid if K⁺ > 5.0.

Peds note: In children < 5 years, diagnosis is clinical. Teach inhaler + spacer technique at every visit.

Acute severity: SpO₂ < 92%, PEFR < 50% predicted, silent chest, tachycardia = severe

Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)
PaediatricsICD-10: J05.0
Peds pearl: Barking cough + stridor + hoarseness. Do NOT examine throat if epiglottitis suspected.
Westley Score Guide
Mild (0–2): Barking cough only. Single dose dexamethasone at home.
Moderate (3–5): Stridor at rest, retractions. Dexamethasone + nebulised adrenaline in ED.
Severe (> 6): ICU / PICU referral. May need intubation.

Dexamethasone
Corticosteroid — All severity levels
0.15 mg/kg oral/IM (max 10 mg). Single dose.
Nebulised Adrenaline (Epinephrine)
Moderate–severe croup

Bronchiolitis
PaediatricsICD-10: J21.0
Key message: Management is SUPPORTIVE — bronchodilators, steroids, and antibiotics are NOT routinely indicated.
Diagnosis & Red Flags
Typical: Age < 2 years, winter/spring, viral prodrome → wheeze + subcostal recession
Apnoea risk: Age < 6 weeks, ex-preterm, SpO₂ < 90%
Feeding: Unable to complete feeds (< 50% normal) = admission threshold
Supportive Management
Supplemental Oxygen
Target SpO₂ ≥ 90%
Low-flow O₂ via nasal prongs 0.5–2 L/min first.
High-flow Nasal Cannula (HFNC)
Moderate-severe respiratory support
Starting flow: 2 L/kg/min (max 60 L/min). Titrate to work of breathing.

Diagnosis by Age

3 months – 3 years: Clean catch urine. Send MSU before antibiotics
Imaging: USS within 6 weeks (all first febrile UTIs)

Oral — Uncomplicated
4 mg/kg BD (max 200 mg BD) × 3–7 days.
Ceftriaxone IV

Bacterial Meningitis
PaediatricsEmergency
Time critical: Do NOT delay antibiotics for LP if signs of sepsis. Give IV antibiotics immediately if non-blanching rash.
Diagnosis
Classic triad: Fever + neck stiffness + photophobia. Non-blanching rash = meningococcal until proven otherwise
Infant signs: Bulging fontanelle, high-pitched cry, poor feeding, irritability
LP CSF: Bacterial: turbid, WBC >1000 (neutrophils), low glucose, high protein
Emergency Treatment
Ceftriaxone IV
Empirical antibiotic — First-line (> 3 months)
80–100 mg/kg IV OD (max 4 g/day) × 7–14 days.
Dexamethasone
Adjunctive — Reduces neurological sequelae
0.15 mg/kg IV q6h × 4 days. Start with or before first antibiotic dose.

Activate NOW: 12-lead ECG within 10 min. STEMI criteria → activate cath lab immediately. Call cardiology.

IV access × 2: Bloods (FBC, U&E, troponin, clotting, G&S, lipids)
Look for: LBBB, posterior MI (V7–V9), RV MI (V3R–V4R)

DAPT loading — Give immediately

VF / Pulseless VT
Cardiac ArrestALS Protocol
ALS 2021: Every 2-minute CPR cycle. Defibrillate ASAP. Adrenaline every 3–5 min (after 3rd shock). Amiodarone after 3rd shock.

Start CPR: 30:2. Rate 100–120/min. Depth 5–6 cm. Rotate every 2 min.
Shock 1: 200 J (biphasic). Resume CPR immediately.
After Shock 3: Adrenaline 1 mg + Amiodarone 300 mg IV/IO.
4H/4T: Hypoxia, Hypovolaemia, Hyper/hypoK, Hypothermia / Tension pneumo, Tamponade, Toxins, Thrombosis

Antiarrhythmic — After 3rd shock

Acute Pulmonary Oedema
Cardiac EmergencyICD-10: J81
Position + oxygen first: Sit patient upright, legs dependent. High-flow O₂ or NIV (CPAP) immediately. GTN if SBP ≥ 100. Furosemide IV. Avoid over-diuresis in cardiogenic shock.
Assessment & Severity
Clinical: Acute dyspnoea, pink frothy sputum, fine bibasal crackles, wheeze ("cardiac asthma"), cold clammy peripheries
Triggers: STEMI, new AF, hypertensive crisis, fluid overload, mitral/aortic valve disease, sepsis
Investigations: CXR (bat-wing oedema, Kerley B), ECG, BNP, ABG, troponin, BMP, echo urgently
Cardiogenic shock: SBP < 90 + poor perfusion → inotropes, IABP, ICU. Avoid GTN + furosemide combination.
Treatment Protocol
CPAP / NIV
Non-invasive respiratory support — First-line
CPAP 5–10 cmH₂O. Reduces preload and improves oxygenation. Use immediately if SpO₂ < 90% or severe distress.
CPAP reduces intubation rates and mortality (ARISE trial). Contraindicated if vomiting/reduced consciousness.
GTN (Glyceryl Trinitrate)
Vasodilator — SBP ≥ 100 mmHg
Sublingual 400 µg STAT, repeat in 5 min. IV GTN 10–200 µg/min infusion if persisting. Titrate to response.
Primarily venodilator → reduces preload rapidly. Avoid if SBP < 90 or RV failure/STEMI.
Furosemide IV
Loop diuretic
40–80 mg IV STAT (use 2–2.5× usual oral dose). If no IV: 40 mg IM. Repeat at 1h if inadequate response.
Venodilatory effect precedes diuresis. Target urine output 200 mL/h. Monitor K⁺ closely.
Morphine
Adjunct — Use cautiously
2.5–5 mg IV slowly. Reduces preload and anxiety. Avoid if respiratory rate < 12/min or CO₂ retention.
Controversial — some evidence of harm. Use only if extreme distress and bronchospasm not dominant feature.
Anaphylaxis
Allergic EmergencyICD-10: T78.2
ADRENALINE FIRST: Give IM adrenaline immediately. Do NOT wait for investigations. Position supine with legs elevated.
Immediate Management
Recognition: Sudden onset ≥ 2 systems (skin, respiratory, CV, GI) after allergen exposure
Call for help: Crash team. Remove trigger.
Airway: High-flow O₂ 15 L/min via NRB mask. Early intubation if laryngeal oedema.
Drug Protocol
Adrenaline (Epinephrine)
FIRST-LINE — IM immediately
Adult: 0.5 mg IM (anterolateral thigh). Child >30 kg: 0.3 mg; <30 kg: 0.15 mg. Repeat at 5 min.
IV Fluid Resuscitation
Vasodilation / Shock
500–1000 mL 0.9% saline IV over 5–10 min.
Chlorphenamine + Hydrocortisone
Adjuncts — After adrenaline
Chlorphenamine 10 mg IV. Hydrocortisone 200 mg IV.
Tension Pneumothorax
Respiratory Emergency
DO NOT WAIT FOR CXR: Clinical diagnosis → immediate needle decompression. Signs: tracheal deviation, absent breath sounds, hypotension, distended neck veins.
Immediate Procedure
Needle decompression: 2nd ICS MCL or 4th/5th ICS AAL. Insert 14G cannula.
Definitive: Chest drain — 4th or 5th ICS, AAL. Seldinger or surgical technique.
If ventilated: Disconnect ventilator first, then decompress.
Supportive Care
High-flow Oxygen
All patients
15 L/min via NRB mask. Accelerates pneumothorax absorption.
IV Analgesia
Pain management
Morphine 2.5–5 mg IV titrated OR fentanyl 25–50 µg IV if haemodynamically unstable.
Massive Pulmonary Embolism
Respiratory / Cardiac EmergencyICD-10: I26.0
Haemodynamic instability + PE: Systemic thrombolysis if no contraindication. Do NOT delay — massive PE carries 30–50% mortality without treatment. Heparin while thrombolysis considered.
Recognition & Workup
Massive PE: SBP < 90 or drop > 40 mmHg + signs of RV failure + PE on imaging
Clinical: Sudden dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, haemoptysis, syncope, tachycardia, raised JVP
CTPA: Definitive imaging. Bedside echo if unstable (RV dilation, D-sign, McConnell's sign)
ECG: Sinus tachycardia commonest. S1Q3T3, RBBB, RV strain pattern
Treatment Protocol
Alteplase (rt-PA)
Systemic thrombolysis — Massive PE
100 mg IV over 2h. If cardiac arrest: 50 mg IV bolus. CPR may continue during infusion.
Absolute contraindications: haemorrhagic stroke < 3 months, active bleeding, major surgery < 10 days. Relatively contraindicated in pregnancy.
UFH
Anticoagulation — Bridge/if thrombolysis contraindicated
80 U/kg IV bolus, then 18 U/kg/hr. Titrate to aPTT 60–100 s.
Do NOT give LMWH in massive PE — unpredictable absorption in shock. Use UFH.
Fluid resuscitation
Haemodynamic support — Cautious
250–500 mL crystalloid challenge. Avoid overload — worsens RV dilation. Use vasopressors (noradrenaline) early if no response.
Airway Foreign Body
Airway EmergencyPeds + Adults
Choking — complete obstruction: Adult/child > 1 yr: up to 5 back blows then 5 abdominal thrusts (Heimlich). Infant: 5 back blows + 5 chest thrusts. If unconscious → CPR + look in mouth before each breath.
Recognition & Action
Mild (effective cough): Encourage coughing. Do NOT intervene physically. Monitor closely.
Severe (ineffective cough): Witnesses sudden onset choking. Cyanosis, cannot speak, weak cough → act immediately.

Severe Hypoglycaemia
Metabolic EmergencyICD-10: E16.0
Glucose < 3 mmol/L + symptoms: If conscious → oral glucose 15–20g fast-acting. If unconscious or IV access → dextrose IV immediately. Never give oral glucose to unconscious patient. Check glucose at 15 min.
Recognition & Causes
Symptoms: Sweating, tremor, palpitations (adrenergic); confusion, slurred speech, seizure, coma (neuroglycopenic)
GP common causes: Insulin overdose, sulphonylurea (glipizide/glibenclamide), missed meals, alcohol, sepsis
Whipple's triad: Low glucose + symptoms + resolution with glucose treatment
Prolonged hypoglycaemia (sulphonylurea): Admit and observe 24h — rebound hypoglycaemia common
Treatment Protocol
Oral Glucose (conscious patient)
First-line — Mild/moderate
15–20g fast-acting glucose: 150–200 mL fruit juice, 4–5 glucose tablets, or 5 jelly babies. Recheck in 15 min. Follow with complex carbohydrate snack.
Dextrose 10% IV

150–200 mL 10% dextrose (= 15–20g glucose) IV over 10–15 min. Recheck glucose at 15 min. Use 50% dextrose ONLY if no other available — 50 mL max via large bore IV.
10% dextrose preferred over 50% — less vein irritation and safer. Avoid extravasation.
Glucagon IM/SC
No IV access — Community/pre-hospital
1 mg IM/SC (adult). 0.5 mg if < 25 kg. Intranasal glucagon 3 mg if available.
Ineffective in liver disease (depleted glycogen), starvation, or alcohol excess. Recovery within 10 min — then give oral carbs.
Hyperkalaemia
Metabolic EmergencyICD-10: E87.5
K⁺ > 6.5 or ECG changes → act immediately: Calcium gluconate IV first (membrane stabilisation). Then drive K⁺ into cells (insulin + dextrose). Then remove K⁺ (diuresis, resonium, dialysis). Do NOT treat pseudo-hyperkalaemia (haemolysis) — recheck.
ECG Changes & Classification
ECG progression: Peaked T waves → prolonged PR → wide QRS → sine wave pattern → VF/asystole
Mild: K⁺ 5.5–6.0 — monitor, treat cause, dietary K restriction
Moderate: K⁺ 6.0–6.5 or symptoms — insulin-dextrose + salbutamol nebulisation
Severe: K⁺ > 6.5 or ECG changes — calcium gluconate STAT + urgent nephrology
Common causes: CKD, ACEi/ARB/MRA, NSAIDS, rhabdomyolysis, tumour lysis, Addison's
Treatment Protocol
Calcium Gluconate 10%
Membrane stabiliser — STEP 1 (ECG changes or K⁺ > 6.5)
10 mL of 10% calcium gluconate IV over 5–10 min. Repeat if ECG unchanged at 5 min. Continuous ECG monitoring. Onset: 1–3 min. Duration: 30–60 min.
Does NOT lower K⁺. Only protects the myocardium. Must be followed by K⁺-lowering treatments.
Actrapid (Insulin) + 50% Dextrose
Intracellular shift — STEP 2
10 units Actrapid IV + 50 mL 50% dextrose (25g) IV over 15–30 min. Lowers K⁺ by 0.5–1.5 mmol/L. Monitor glucose hourly × 6h (hypoglycaemia risk).
Effect starts within 15–30 min, lasts 4–6h. If glucose ≥ 15 mmol/L, give insulin alone initially.
Salbutamol nebulised
Intracellular shift — Adjunct to Step 2
10–20 mg nebulised (2.5–5× standard bronchodilator dose). Lowers K⁺ by further 0.5–1.0 mmol/L.
Additive to insulin effect. Useful if IV access difficult. Avoid in ischaemic heart disease (tachycardia).
Furosemide / Sodium Resonium / Dialysis
K⁺ removal — STEP 3
Furosemide 40–80 mg IV if renal function preserved. Sodium resonium 15–30 g PO (acts in hours). Urgent dialysis for renal failure or severe/refractory hyperkalaemia.

Labs: Leucocytosis (WBC > 10) + ↑ CRP. Normal WBC does NOT exclude appendicitis.
Imaging: USS first-line in children/pregnancy. CT abdomen/pelvis if diagnosis uncertain in adults.
Pre-operative Management
Analgesia
Do NOT withhold
Paracetamol 1 g IV + Morphine 2.5–5 mg IV titrated. Adequate analgesia does NOT obscure diagnosis.
Antibiotics (if perforation/abscess)
Pre-op prophylaxis / conservative management
Co-amoxiclav 1.2 g IV TDS or Ceftriaxone 2 g IV + Metronidazole 500 mg IV TDS.
Antibiotics-first approach may be considered for uncomplicated appendicitis (CODA trial), but surgery remains standard.
NPO + IV Fluids
Preparation for surgery
Nil by mouth. 0.9% saline 1–1.5 L/24h. Early surgical referral.
Hypotension & Shock
Critical EmergencyMAP < 65 = Organ Hypoperfusion
Shock types (4 H's): Hypovolaemic, Haemorrhagic, Cardiogenic, Distributive (sepsis/anaphylaxis). Treat cause while supporting perfusion. Lactate > 2 mmol/L = tissue hypoxia.
Rapid Assessment
ABCs + Monitoring: High-flow O₂, IV access × 2, ECG, SpO₂, BP q5min, urine output.
Bedside tests: Glucose, lactate, ABG, ECG, FAST scan (trauma), pregnancy test.
Identify source: Haemorrhage? Sepsis? MI? PE? Anaphylaxis? Adrenal crisis?
Resuscitation Protocol
Crystalloid Bolus
First-line fluid resuscitation
0.9% saline or Balanced crystalloid 500–1000 mL IV over 15 min. Reassess after each bolus.
Avoid excessive fluids in cardiogenic shock. Use passive leg raise to assess fluid responsiveness.
Vasopressors
If hypotension persists after fluids
Norepinephrine 0.01–3 µg/kg/min via central line. Target MAP ≥ 65 mmHg.
Start early in septic shock. Add vasopressin 0.03 U/min if refractory.
Blood Products
Haemorrhagic shock
Activate massive transfusion protocol if ongoing bleeding. Target Hb > 70 g/L (higher if CAD).
Corrosive Ingestion
Toxicology EmergencyICD-10: T54
DO NOT induce vomiting or give charcoal: Risk of re-exposure and perforation. Do NOT neutralise (exothermic reaction). Assess airway first — stridor = impending obstruction → early intubation.
Immediate Actions
Airway: Inspect oropharynx. Drooling, stridor, hoarseness = upper airway injury → call anaesthetics early.
Decontamination: Remove contaminated clothing. Rinse mouth/skin with water. Do NOT give anything by mouth.
Imaging: CXR if respiratory symptoms (perforation/aspiration). CT if severe pain/peritonism.
Supportive & Definitive Care

Symptom control
Morphine 2.5–5 mg IV titrated. Ondansetron 4 mg IV. Avoid NSAIDs (GI irritation).
IV PPI
Gastric protection
Pantoprazole 40 mg IV OD. May reduce stricture formation (evidence limited).
Early Endoscopy
Risk stratification — 12–24h
Gastroscopy to grade injury (Zargar classification). Grade 2b/3 = high perforation risk → surgical consult.
Contraindicated if perforation suspected. Steroids for oesophageal strictures remain controversial.
Zinc Phosphide Poisoning
Toxicology EmergencyMortality 30–70%
Garlic odour + phosphine gas: Releases phosphine (PH₃) in stomach — toxic to heart, lungs, liver. No specific antidote. Supportive care is critical. Staff protection: avoid inhalation of patient's breath/vomit.
Clinical Features
Early (0–6h): Nausea, vomiting (garlic odour), abdominal pain, agitation.
Delayed (6–24h): Shock, ARDS, myocarditis, hepatic/renal failure, metabolic acidosis.
Labs: ABG (metabolic acidosis), LFTs, creatinine, troponin, lactate. ECG: QT prolongation, arrhythmias.
Emergency Management

Only if < 1h + protected airway
Consider only in massive ingestion with RSI. Use large-bore tube. Avoid if corrosive co-ingestion.
No role for activated charcoal (poor adsorption). Do NOT give oils/fats (increase phosphine absorption).
Supportive ICU Care
Mainstay of treatment
IV fluids, vasopressors for shock, mechanical ventilation for ARDS, correct acidosis (NaHCO₃), monitor for arrhythmias.

Diagnostic Criteria
History: Clear trigger + prodrome + rapid recovery + no injury. Normal ECG.
Exclusion: No chest pain, palpitations, exertional onset, family history of sudden death.
Orthostatic testing: May reproduce symptoms. BP/HR drop on standing supports diagnosis.

Immediate recovery

Honey-coloured crusts: Highly contagious. Localised → topical. Widespread/bullous → oral antibiotics. Exclude from school until 48h of treatment.
Diagnostic Approach
Clinical: Swab crusted lesions if bullous or widespread to rule out MRSA.
Treatment Protocol
Mupirocin 2% Ointment
Localised (5 days)
Apply TID × 5 days. Cover 1 cm beyond lesion.
Flucloxacillin Oral
Widespread (7 days)
500 mg QID × 7 days. Clarithromycin if penicillin allergic.
Cellulitis (Mild)
Skin & soft tissue infection
Non-purulent cellulitis: Usually β-haemolytic strep. Erythema, warmth, swelling, tenderness. Mark borders to monitor spread. Elevate limb.
Diagnostic Approach
Clinical: Differentiate from DVT (usually no fever/erythema), contact dermatitis (bilateral/itchy).
Treatment Protocol
Flucloxacillin
First-line (5–7 days)
500 mg QID × 5–7 days.
Cephalexin / Clindamycin
Alternatives
Cephalexin 500 mg QID or Clindamycin 300 mg TID (penicillin allergy).
Furunculosis
Boils / Carbuncles
S. aureus infection of hair follicle: Painful, fluctuant nodule. I&D is primary treatment. Do NOT squeeze face (cavernous sinus thrombosis risk).
Diagnostic Approach
Clinical: Palpate for fluctuance. If recurrent, screen for MRSA carriage (nose/groin swabs).
Treatment Protocol
Incision & Drainage
Primary treatment
Elliptical incision. Pack cavity. Send pus for culture.
Oral Antibiotics
If cellulitis / systemic signs
Flucloxacillin 500 mg QID or Doxycycline 100 mg BID (MRSA risk) × 7 days.
Herpes Zoster (Shingles)
VZV reactivation
Start antivirals within 72h: Reduces duration & PHN risk. Dermatomal vesicular rash. Immunocompromised or ocic involvement → urgent referral.
Diagnostic Approach
Clinical: Classic dermatomal distribution. PCR of vesicle fluid if atypical.
Treatment Protocol
Valacyclovir
First-line (7 days)
1 g TID × 7 days. Better bioavailability than acyclovir.
Neuropathic Pain Agents
PHN prevention
Amitriptyline 10 mg nocte or Gabapentin 100 mg nocte. Titrate as needed.
Eczema / Dermatitis
Inflammatory skin
Cornerstone: Emollients 3–4×/day. Avoid triggers (fragrance, wool, heat). Step up steroid potency by site/severity.
Diagnostic Approach
Clinical: Pruritus + typical morphology/distribution. Rule out contact dermatitis (patch test) or fungal infection (KOH).
Treatment Protocol
Topical Corticosteroids
Flares (7–14 days)
Mild: Hydrocortisone 1% (face). Moderate: Betamethasone 0.025% (body). Apply thinly OD.
Emollients
Maintenance
Liberal application. Use soap substitutes for bathing.
Atopic Dermatitis
Chronic relapsing
Atopic triad: Eczema, asthma, allergic rhinitis. Pruritus is primary. Treat flares aggressively, maintain with emollients. Filaggrin mutation → barrier defect.
Diagnostic Approach
Clinical + History: Hanifin & Rajka criteria: pruritus, typical morphology, chronicity, personal/family atopy.
Treatment Protocol
Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors
Face/neck/sensitive areas
Tacrolimus 0.1% ointment BID. No skin atrophy risk.
Dupilumab
Severe refractory (Biologic)
300 mg SC q2wks. IL-4/IL-13 inhibitor. Dermatology referral.
Contact Dermatitis
Irritant / Allergic
Irritant: Direct damage (detergents). Allergic: Type IV hypersensitivity (nickel, rubber). Sharply demarcated to exposure site. Patch testing if chronic.
Diagnostic Approach
History: Temporal relationship to exposure. Patch testing for allergic type (read at 48h & 96h).
Treatment Protocol
Avoidance + Barriers
Primary treatment
Identify & remove trigger. Nitrile gloves, barrier creams.
Topical Steroids
Acute flare
Potent steroid (Betamethasone 0.05%) × 7–14 days. Taper rapidly once clear.
Urticaria
Wheals / Hives
Acute (< 6 wks) vs Chronic (> 6 wks): Often viral/idiopathic acute. Chronic: autoimmune, physical triggers, NSAIDs. Individual lesions last < 24h. If angioedema → treat as anaphylaxis.
Diagnostic Approach
Clinical: Transient wheals + pruritus. Dermatographism test. ESR/CRP if chronic to rule out systemic disease.
Treatment Protocol
Non-Sedating H1 Antihistamines
First-line (up-dose if needed)
Cetirizine 10 mg OD or Fexofenadine 180 mg OD. Can increase to 4× dose in chronic.
Omalizumab
Chronic spontaneous (refractory)
300 mg SC q4wks. Anti-IgE. Allergy clinic referral.
Viral URTI
Common cold
Self-limiting: 7–10 days. Green sputum = neutrophils, NOT necessarily bacterial. Antibiotics DO NOT help. Supportive care only.
Diagnostic Approach
Clinical: Coryza, sore throat, mild cough, low-grade fever. No focal chest signs.
Treatment Protocol
Analgesia + Decongestants
Symptomatic
Paracetamol/Ibuprofen PRN. Xylometazoline nasal spray (max 5–7 days).
Honey / Fluids
Evidence-based cough relief
1–2 tsp honey (adults/kids >1yr). Rest + hydration.
Acute Pharyngitis / Tonsillitis
Strep vs Viral
Use Centor Score: Most are viral. Antibiotics only if Centor ≥ 3 or confirmed GAS. Delayed prescribing reduces unnecessary use.
Diagnostic Approach
Centor Criteria: Fever, exudate, tender nodes, no cough, age. Throat swab if uncertain.
Treatment Protocol
Penicillin V
First-line if GAS+ (10 days)
500 mg QID × 10 days. Prevents rheumatic fever.
Azithromycin
Penicillin allergy
500 mg OD × 3 days.
Acute Sinusitis
Viral vs Bacterial
Bacterial criteria: Symptoms > 10 days without improvement, severe onset (fever + purulent discharge × 3–4d), or "double sickening". Most are viral.
Diagnostic Approach
Clinical: Facial pain, purulent rhinorrhoea, congestion. Imaging only if complications suspected.
Treatment Protocol
Intranasal Steroids
All cases (14 days)
Fluticasone/Mometasone 2 sprays/nostril OD.
Amoxicillin
Bacterial (5–7 days)
500 mg TID. Co-amoxiclav if severe/recurrent.
Acute Bronchitis
Cough < 3 weeks
Usually viral: Antibiotics NOT recommended unless COPD exacerbation or pertussis suspected. Cough can last 2–3 weeks. Reassure.
Diagnostic Approach
Clinical: Productive cough, clear CXR. Exclude pneumonia (fever, focal crackles, tachypnoea).
Treatment Protocol
Supportive Care
Mainstay
Paracetamol, honey, fluids. Salbutamol inhaler if wheezing.
Community-Acquired Pneumonia (Mild)
CURB-65 ≤ 1
Outpatient management: Normal vitals, able to hydrate. Treat empirically for S. pneumoniae & atypicals. Follow up at 48h.
Diagnostic Approach
Confirm: Focal crackles/bronchial breathing + new consolidation on CXR. CRP ↑↑.
Treatment Protocol
Amoxicillin + Clarithromycin
Standard OPD (5–7 days)
Amoxicillin 1g TID + Clarithromycin 500mg BID.
Doxycycline
Alternative / Macrolide resistant
100 mg OD × 7 days. Covers atypicals well.
Allergic Rhinitis
Seasonal / Perennial
Classic triad: Sneezing, clear rhinorrhoea, nasal itch. Allergic shiners (dark circles). Nasal polyps common. Intranasal steroids are most effective.
Diagnostic Approach
Clinical + History: Trigger identification. Skin prick testing or specific IgE if refractory.
Treatment Protocol
Intranasal Corticosteroids
First-line
Fluticasone/Mometasone 2 sprays/nostril OD. Take consistently, not PRN.
Oral Antihistamines
Mild intermittent
Cetirizine 10 mg OD. Non-sedating preferred.
Tension Headache
Most common primary headache
Characteristics: Band-like, bilateral, pressing/tightening. NOT worsened by activity. No nausea/vomiting.
Diagnostic Approach
Clinical: Exclude red flags (SNOOP10). Normal neuro exam.
Treatment Protocol
Simple Analgesics
Acute
Paracetamol 1g or Ibuprofen 400mg at onset. Limit to < 15 days/month.
Amitriptyline
Prophylaxis (Frequent TTH)

Migraine
With / Without aura
Treat early: Take acute medication at pain onset. Limit to ≤ 2 days/week to avoid rebound headache. Triptans for moderate-severe attacks.
Diagnostic Approach
ICHD-3 Criteria: 5+ attacks, 4–72h duration, unilateral, pulsating, moderate-severe, worsened by activity + nausea/photophobia.
Treatment Protocol
NSAID + Antiemetic
Mild-moderate
Ibuprofen 600mg + Metoclopramide 10mg at onset.
Triptans
Moderate-severe
Sumatriptan 50mg PO or Rizatriptan 10mg. Contraindicated in CAD.

Prevention
Indications: ≥ 4 attacks/month, severe disability, acute meds failing/overused. Trial for 3–6 months.
Diagnostic Approach
Headache diary: Track frequency, triggers, medication use.

Propranolol
First-line
40–80 mg BID. Avoid in asthma.
Topiramate / Amitriptyline
Alternatives
Topiramate 25→50mg BID (teratogenic). Amitriptyline 10mg nocte.
Vertigo (BPPV)
Benign Paroxysmal Positional
Classic: Brief (< 1 min) vertigo triggered by head position changes. No hearing loss. Diagnose with Dix-Hallpike test. Treat with Epley.
Diagnostic Approach
Dix-Hallpike: Latent, fatigable torsional nystagmus. Differentiate from Meniere's (hearing loss/tinnitus) or vestibular neuritis (constant vertigo).
Treatment Protocol
Epley Manoeuvre
First-line cure
Canalith repositioning sequence. Repeat 2–3× per session. Cure rate > 80%.
Prochlorperazine
Symptomatic (≤ 3 days)
5 mg TDS. Do NOT use long-term (delays vestibular compensation).
Iron Deficiency Anemia
Microcytic Hypochromic
Find the cause: In men/postmenopausal women: GI blood loss until proven otherwise. Ferritin < 30 µg/L diagnostic.
Diagnostic Approach
Labs: Low Hb, low MCV, low ferritin, high TIBC. Reticulocyte count ↑ after treatment starts. Coeliac screen + endoscopy/colonoscopy if source unclear.
Treatment Protocol
Ferrous Sulfate
Oral iron
200 mg TID (65mg elemental/tab). Take with Vitamin C. Avoid tea/coffee/calcium × 2h.
IV Iron
Intolerance / malabsorption / severe
Ferric carboxymaltose 750–1000 mg IV. Rapid repletion, bypasses GI side effects.
Hypertriglyceridemia
CV / Pancreatitis risk
Severe (> 500 mg/dL): High risk of acute pancreatitis → treat urgently. Mild-mod: manage CV risk factors first.
Diagnostic Approach
Fasting Lipids: TG ≥ 150 mg/dL. Exclude secondary causes: uncontrolled DM, alcohol, hypothyroidism, retinoids, thiazides.
Treatment Protocol
Fibrates
Most effective for TG
Fenofibrate 145 mg OD. Lowers TG by 30–50%.
Prescription Omega-3
Adjunct
Icosapent ethyl 2 g BID. Proven CV benefit in high-risk patients (REDUCE-IT).
Hypercholesterolemia (High LDL)
ASCVD risk reduction
Treat to target: Very high risk: LDL < 1.4 mmol/L & ↓50%. High risk: LDL < 1.8 mmol/L. Use statin intensity based on risk.
Diagnostic Approach
Fasting Lipid Profile: Calculate 10-year ASCVD risk (e.g., PREVENT score). Rule out secondary: hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, cholestasis.
Treatment Protocol
High-Intensity Statin
First-line
Atorvastatin 40–80 mg OD or Rosuvastatin 20–40 mg OD. Check LFTs at baseline & 3 months.
Ezetimibe
Add-on if goal not met
10 mg OD. Lowers LDL further 15–20%.
