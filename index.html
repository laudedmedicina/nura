Heart Failure
HFrEF / HFpEFICD-10: I50⚠️ High Mortality
🔬 Clinical pearl: BNP > 400 pg/mL or NT-proBNP > 900 pg/mL strongly supports acute decompensated HF. Always assess ejection fraction — management differs significantly between HFrEF (EF < 40%) and HFpEF (EF ≥ 50%).
🔍 Diagnostic Criteria
Framingham Criteria: 2 major or 1 major + 2 minor required for diagnosis
Echo: Essential — assess EF, wall motion, valvular disease, diastolic dysfunction (E/e' ratio)
Labs: BNP/NT-proBNP, BMP, LFTs, CBC, TSH, iron studies
CXR: Cardiomegaly, Kerley B lines, bilateral pleural effusions, pulmonary edema
ECG: LVH, LBBB — LBBB with EF < 35% triggers CRT consideration
💊 First-Line Drugs — HFrEF
Sacubitril / Valsartan
ARNI — Cornerstone therapy
Start: 24/26 mg BID → target 97/103 mg BID. Replace ACEi/ARB.
Reduces mortality by 20% vs enalapril (PARADIGM-HF). Washout 36h from ACEi before starting.
Carvedilol / Metoprolol succinate
Beta-blocker
Carvedilol: 3.125 mg BID → 25–50 mg BID. Start only when euvolemic.
Never initiate in acute decompensation. Titrate slowly over weeks.
Empagliflozin / Dapagliflozin
SGLT2 Inhibitor — "Fourth Pillar"
Empagliflozin 10 mg OD. Benefit regardless of DM status.
Reduces HF hospitalization and CV death (EMPEROR-Reduced).
Spironolactone / Eplerenone
MRA
Spironolactone 25 mg OD. Monitor K⁺ and creatinine closely.
Avoid if K⁺ > 5.0 or eGFR < 30. Eplerenone preferred post-MI.
Atrial Fibrillation
Paroxysmal / Persistent / PermanentICD-10: I48
📏 Rule of thumb: Always calculate CHA₂DS₂-VASc before anticoagulation. Score ≥ 2 (males) or ≥ 3 (females) → anticoagulate.
🔍 Diagnostic Tips
ECG: Irregularly irregular rhythm, absent P waves, fibrillatory baseline
Classify duration: Paroxysmal (< 7 days), persistent (> 7 days), long-standing (> 1 year), permanent
TTE: LV function, valvular disease, LA size (> 45 mm → less likely cardioversion success)
Labs: TSH (thyrotoxicosis!), electrolytes, renal function, CBC
💊 Pharmacotherapy
Apixaban
DOAC — Anticoagulation (preferred)
5 mg BID (2.5 mg BID if ≥ 2 of: age ≥ 80, weight ≤ 60 kg, Cr ≥ 1.5)
ARISTOTLE: Superior to warfarin in stroke prevention and major bleeding.
Bisoprolol / Metoprolol
Beta-blocker — Rate control
Bisoprolol 2.5–10 mg OD. Target HR < 80 bpm at rest.
Amiodarone
Class III — Rhythm control
Loading: 200 mg TID × 4 wks → 200 mg OD maintenance
Most effective antiarrhythmic but significant toxicity. Regular monitoring essential.
Acute Coronary Syndrome
STEMI / NSTEMI / UAICD-10: I21–I24
⏱️ STEMI rule: Door-to-balloon time < 90 min. Aspirin 300 mg + Ticagrelor 180 mg loading before transfer.
🔍 Diagnostic Tips
STEMI ECG: ≥ 1 mm ST elevation in ≥ 2 contiguous limb leads or ≥ 2 mm precordial, new LBBB
Troponin: High-sensitivity — measure at 0h and 1–3h. Rise and/or fall pattern is diagnostic
Posterior MI: ST depression V1–V3 with dominant R — do posterior leads (V7–V9)
RV MI: Inferior STEMI + hypotension — check right-sided leads (V3R–V4R)
💊 Dual Antiplatelet + Therapy
Aspirin + Ticagrelor
DAPT — Standard of care
Aspirin 300 mg load → 75–100 mg OD. Ticagrelor 180 mg load → 90 mg BID
Continue DAPT for 12 months post-ACS.
Enoxaparin / UFH
Anticoagulation — Acute phase
Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg SC BID (adjust for eGFR). UFH if PCI imminent.
High-intensity Statin
HMG-CoA Reductase Inhibitor
Atorvastatin 80 mg OD or Rosuvastatin 40 mg OD. Start within 24h.
Target LDL < 1.4 mmol/L. Add ezetimibe if not at goal.
Hypertension
Essential / SecondaryICD-10: I10
⚡ Hypertensive emergency: BP > 180/120 with end-organ damage → reduce MAP by ≤ 25% in first hour with IV agents. Do NOT normalize acutely.
🔍 Diagnostic Tips
Confirm: ABPM preferred — diagnoses white-coat and masked HTN
Secondary causes: Young patients, resistant HTN — screen CKD, primary aldosteronism, renovascular, OSA, pheo
End-organ damage: Fundoscopy, ECG (LVH), urinalysis (proteinuria), eGFR, ABI
💊 Stepwise Pharmacotherapy
Amlodipine
CCB — First-line
5–10 mg OD. Well tolerated. Preferred in elderly and Black patients.
Side effect: peripheral edema. No monitoring required.
Ramipril / Perindopril
ACE Inhibitor
Ramipril 2.5–10 mg OD. Monitor K⁺ and creatinine. Avoid in pregnancy.
Indapamide
Thiazide-like diuretic
1.5 mg SR OD. Fewer metabolic effects than hydrochlorothiazide.
Spironolactone
MRA — Resistant HTN (4th line)
25–50 mg OD. Most effective add-on. Monitor K⁺ closely.
Asthma
Intermittent / PersistentICD-10: J45
⚠️ Acute severe warning: PEFR < 50%, silent chest, cyanosis → nebulized salbutamol + IV magnesium sulfate + systemic corticosteroids. Call ICU early.
🔍 Diagnostic Tips
Spirometry: FEV₁/FVC < 0.7 with ≥ 12% reversibility after bronchodilator confirms diagnosis
FeNO: > 50 ppb suggests eosinophilic inflammation — guides steroid response
Allergy workup: Skin prick test or specific IgE for uncontrolled/severe asthma
💊 Step Therapy (GINA 2024)
Budesonide/Formoterol
ICS/LABA — MART regimen (preferred)
100/6 µg as reliever AND maintenance (SMART therapy). Reduces exacerbations.
GINA recommends ICS-formoterol over SABA alone for all severity levels.
Dupilumab / Mepolizumab
Biologic — Severe eosinophilic asthma
Dupilumab 200–300 mg SC q2wks. Mepolizumab 100 mg SC monthly.
Consider when blood eos ≥ 300/µL or FeNO ≥ 25 ppb with uncontrolled severe asthma.
COPD
GOLD Stage I–IVICD-10: J44
🫁 Acute exacerbation: Prednisolone 40 mg/day × 5 days. Antibiotics if purulent sputum. Target SpO₂ 88–92% — avoid over-oxygenation.
🔍 Diagnostic Tips
Spirometry: Post-bronchodilator FEV₁/FVC < 0.7 confirms airflow limitation
ABG: Chronic hypercapnia (PaCO₂ > 6 kPa) → assess for home NIV candidacy
GOLD ABE assessment: Classify by FEV₁ + symptom burden (mMRC/CAT) + exacerbation history
💊 Pharmacotherapy (GOLD 2024)
Tiotropium (Spiriva)
LAMA — Cornerstone therapy
18 µg OD via HandiHaler or 2.5 µg via Respimat.
FF/UMEC/VI (Trelegy Ellipta)
ICS/LAMA/LABA — Triple therapy
100/62.5/25 µg OD. For frequent exacerbators with blood eos ≥ 300/µL.
IMPACT trial: significant reduction in exacerbations vs dual therapy.
Community-Acquired Pneumonia
Typical / AtypicalICD-10: J18
📊 Severity: CURB-65 guides site-of-care. Score ≥ 3 → hospital. Score ≥ 4 → consider ICU.
🔍 Diagnostic Tips
CXR: Lobar consolidation (typical: S. pneumoniae) vs diffuse interstitial (atypical: Mycoplasma, Legionella)
Legionella: Urine antigen — suspect in severe CAP, hyponatremia, GI symptoms, smokers
PCT: > 0.25 µg/L supports bacterial infection — guides antibiotic duration
💊 Antibiotic Therapy
Amoxicillin + Clarithromycin
Mild-moderate CAP
Amoxicillin 1 g TID + Clarithromycin 500 mg BID × 5–7 days PO
Pip-Tazo + Azithromycin
Severe CAP / ICU
Pip-Tazo 4.5 g IV TDS + Azithromycin 500 mg IV OD. Add oseltamivir if influenza suspected.
Type 2 Diabetes
T2DMICD-10: E11
📋 2024 ADA approach: In patients with CVD, HF, or CKD: SGLT2i or GLP-1 RA are first-line regardless of HbA1c.
🔍 Diagnostic Tips
Diagnosis: HbA1c ≥ 6.5%, FPG ≥ 7 mmol/L, 2h PG ≥ 11.1 (OGTT)
Complications screening: Annual urine ACR, eGFR, retinal exam, monofilament foot, lipids, BP
HbA1c targets: < 7% most patients; < 6.5% young/no CVD; < 8% elderly/frail
💊 Evidence-Based Pharmacotherapy
Metformin
Biguanide — Foundation therapy
500 mg OD → target 1000 mg BID. Hold if eGFR < 30.
Semaglutide (Ozempic)
GLP-1 RA — Preferred add-on
0.25 mg SC weekly → 0.5 mg → 1 mg. Oral: 3 mg OD escalate monthly.
SUSTAIN-6: 26% ↓ MACE. First-line with ASCVD.
Empagliflozin (Jardiance)
SGLT2 Inhibitor
10–25 mg OD before first meal. Avoid if eGFR < 20.
EMPA-REG: 38% ↓ CV death. Preferred in HFrEF and CKD.
Hypothyroidism
Primary / CentralICD-10: E03
🚨 Myxedema coma: Hypothermia, altered consciousness, bradycardia → IV levothyroxine 200–400 µg loading + liothyronine + IV hydrocortisone.
🔍 Diagnostic Tips
TSH: Best screening. Elevated TSH + low fT4 = primary. Normal/low TSH + low fT4 = central
Hashimoto's: TPO antibodies — most common cause in iodine-sufficient areas
Pregnancy: TSH targets — T1: < 2.5, T2–T3: < 3.0. Screen high-risk women in T1
💊 Pharmacotherapy
Levothyroxine (T4)
Thyroid hormone — First-line
1.6 µg/kg/day. Start low (25–50 µg) in elderly/CAD. Increase by 25 µg every 4–6 wks.
Take fasting, 30–60 min before breakfast. Avoid concurrent iron, calcium, PPIs.
Liver Cirrhosis
Compensated / DecompensatedICD-10: K74
🔬 Variceal prophylaxis: All cirrhosis → endoscopy at diagnosis. Large varices → NSBB (propranolol/carvedilol) or band ligation.
🔍 Diagnostic Tips
FibroScan: LSM > 12.5 kPa suggests cirrhosis. Non-invasive.
Labs: Thrombocytopenia + raised INR + hypoalbuminaemia = portal hypertension
MELD score: > 15 → transplant evaluation. > 25 → high urgency
💊 Pharmacotherapy by Complication
Spironolactone + Furosemide
Diuretics — Ascites
Spiro 100 mg : Furo 40 mg ratio (titrate together). Max weight loss: 500 g/day.
Restrict Na < 90 mmol/day. Stop if Na⁺ < 125 (HRS risk).
Rifaximin + Lactulose
Hepatic encephalopathy prevention
Rifaximin 550 mg BID + Lactulose 15–30 mL BID–TID. Target 2–3 soft stools/day.
GERD / Reflux Disease
NERD / ERD / Barrett'sICD-10: K21
⚠️ Alarm features: Dysphagia, weight loss, haematemesis, age > 55 with new dyspepsia → urgent endoscopy.
🔍 Diagnostic Tips
Empirical PPI trial: 4–8 weeks is diagnostic and therapeutic for typical symptoms
24h pH-impedance: Gold standard for reflux quantification — useful in PPI non-responders
💊 Pharmacotherapy
Omeprazole / Lansoprazole
PPI — First-line
Omeprazole 20–40 mg OD, 30–60 min before breakfast. 4–8 week course.
Long-term risks: hypomagnesemia, B12 deficiency, C. diff, fractures. Step down when possible.
Vonoprazan (Voquezna)
P-CAB (Potassium-competitive acid blocker)
10–20 mg OD. Faster onset, not affected by CYP2C19 polymorphism.
Urinary Tract Infection
Uncomplicated / ComplicatedICD-10: N39.0
🚨 Urosepsis: Fever, rigors, flank pain → blood cultures × 2, urine culture, IV antibiotics, sepsis bundle.
🔍 Diagnostic Tips
Dipstick: Nitrites + leucocyte esterase — high NPV if both negative
Do not treat ASB: Asymptomatic bacteriuria — only treat in pregnancy and pre-urological procedures
💊 Antibiotic Therapy
Nitrofurantoin
Uncomplicated lower UTI — First-line
100 mg MR BID × 5 days. Take with food. Avoid if eGFR < 45.
Never use if pyelonephritis suspected — no upper tract coverage.
Ciprofloxacin
Complicated UTI / Pyelonephritis
500 mg BID × 7–10 days (pyelonephritis).
Ceftriaxone / Pip-Tazo
Urosepsis — IV therapy
Ceftriaxone 2 g IV OD or Pip-Tazo 4.5 g IV TDS. De-escalate per sensitivities.
Chronic Kidney Disease
G1–G5 / A1–A3ICD-10: N18
📋 KDIGO 2024: SGLT2 inhibitors recommended for all CKD (eGFR ≥ 20) regardless of diabetes — reduce progression and CV death.
🔍 Diagnostic Tips
CKD staging: CKD-EPI equation for eGFR. Combine GFR category (G1–G5) with albuminuria (A1–A3)
Complications: CKD-MBD (PTH, Ca²⁺, phosphate, vitamin D), anaemia, hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis
💊 Pharmacotherapy
Dapagliflozin (Farxiga)
SGLT2 Inhibitor — Nephroprotective
10 mg OD. Use from eGFR ≥ 20. Continue until dialysis once started.
DAPA-CKD: 44% ↓ risk of sustained ≥ 50% eGFR decline, ESKD, or renal/CV death.
Finerenone (Kerendia)
Non-steroidal MRA
10–20 mg OD (dose by eGFR). Monitor K⁺ at 4 weeks. Avoid if K⁺ > 5.0.
Paediatric Asthma
👶 PaediatricsICD-10: J45Age ≥ 5 years
👶 Peds note: In children < 5 years, diagnosis is clinical. Avoid spacer confusion — teach inhaler technique at every visit. Severe attack in child: call senior early, involve anaesthesia if concerned.
🔍 Diagnostic Tips
Age < 5: Recurrent wheeze with viral triggers — 3+ episodes suggest asthma. Spirometry not reliable < 5 yrs
Age ≥ 5: Spirometry with reversibility — FEV₁ improvement ≥ 12% after salbutamol
Triggers: Allergens, exercise, cold air, viral URTI — identify and minimise at every review
Acute severity: SpO₂ < 92%, PEFR < 50% predicted, silent chest, tachycardia = severe
💊 Step Therapy — Weight-based
Salbutamol (MDI + spacer)
SABA — Reliever (all ages)
Age < 5: 2–4 puffs × 100 µg every 20 min (acute). Age ≥ 5: 4–8 puffs via spacer.
Mask spacer for < 3 years. Mouthpiece spacer for 3–5 years.
Fluticasone propionate
ICS — Preventive
Age 1–4: 50 µg BD (low dose). Age 5–11: 100–200 µg BD. Age ≥ 12: adult dosing.
Prednisolone (oral)
Corticosteroid — Acute exacerbation
1–2 mg/kg/day (max 40 mg) × 3–5 days. No need to taper if < 2 weeks.
IV Magnesium Sulfate
Severe acute asthma
25–75 mg/kg IV (max 2.5 g) over 20 min. Single dose in severe/life-threatening attack.
Use in children > 2 years. Monitor for hypotension and respiratory depression.
Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)
👶 PaediatricsICD-10: J05.06 months – 6 years
👶 Peds pearl: Barking cough + stridor + hoarseness after viral prodrome. Westley Croup Score guides management. Do NOT examine throat if epiglottitis suspected (drooling, tripoding, toxic appearance).
🔍 Westley Score Guide
Mild (Westley 0–2): Barking cough only. Manage at home with single dose dexamethasone.
Moderate (3–5): Stridor at rest, retractions. Dexamethasone + nebulised adrenaline in ED.
Severe (> 6): Stridor at rest + significant retractions + agitation/fatigue. ICU, PICU referral. May need intubation.
Imaging: CXR if atypical — "steeple sign" (subglottic narrowing) on PA view
💊 Treatment Protocol
Dexamethasone
Corticosteroid — All severity levels
0.15 mg/kg oral/IM (max 10 mg). Single dose. Can be given at home by GP.
Oral is as effective as IM. Repeat at 24h if still symptomatic.
Nebulised Adrenaline (Epinephrine)
Moderate–severe croup (hospital only)
0.5 mL/kg of 1:1000 solution (max 5 mL) nebulised. Observe for 2–4h (rebound risk).
Short-acting (1–2h). Always observe after administration — do not discharge early.
Heliox (70:30 He:O₂)
Adjunct — Severe distress
70% helium / 30% oxygen via tight-fitting mask. Reduces airway turbulence.
Bridge to intubation. Not widely available outside tertiary centres.
Bronchiolitis
👶 PaediatricsICD-10: J21.0Age < 2 years
👶 Key message: Bronchiolitis is a CLINICAL diagnosis. Management is supportive — bronchodilators, steroids, and antibiotics are NOT routinely indicated (NICE 2015/AAP 2014).
🔍 Diagnosis & Red Flags
Typical: Age < 2 years, winter/spring, viral prodrome → wheeze + subcostal recession + crackles
Apnoea risk: Age < 6 weeks, ex-preterm, cardiac/respiratory comorbidity, SpO₂ < 90%
Feeding: Unable to complete feeds (< 50% normal) = hospital admission threshold
RSV PCR: Useful for cohorting in hospital. NPA or combined throat/nasal swab.
💊 Supportive Management
Supplemental Oxygen
Target SpO₂ ≥ 90% (≥ 92% in UK)
Low-flow O₂ via nasal prongs 0.5–2 L/min first. Wean as SpO₂ improves.
High-flow Nasal Cannula (HFNC)
Respiratory support — Moderate-severe
Starting flow: 2 L/kg/min (max 60 L/min). FiO₂ 0.3–1.0. Titrate to work of breathing.
Reduces ICU admissions. Escalate to CPAP/intubation if no improvement in 1–2h.
NG tube feeds / IV fluids
Feeding support
If unable to feed safely: NG feeds at maintenance rate. IV fluids if NG not tolerated.
Palivizumab (Synagis)
RSV prophylaxis — High-risk infants only
15 mg/kg IM monthly during RSV season. For: ex-preterm, CHD, chronic lung disease.
NOT treatment — purely preventive. Given before RSV season starts.
Paediatric UTI
👶 PaediatricsICD-10: N39.0
👶 NICE guideline: All children with first febrile UTI need renal USS. DMSA and MCUG depend on age and USS findings. Fever without a source in infant < 3 months → urine by catheter.
🔍 Diagnosis by Age
< 3 months: Any febrile child — urine by catheter. Treat as sepsis until proven otherwise
3 months – 3 years: Clean catch urine. Dipstick first — if positive, send MSU before antibiotics
Atypical UTI features: Seriously ill, poor flow, abdominal mass, raised creatinine, failure to respond in 48h
Imaging: USS within 6 weeks (all first febrile UTIs). DMSA at 4–6 months if atypical/recurrent
💊 Antibiotic Therapy — Weight-based
Trimethoprim
Oral — Uncomplicated (check local resistance)
Age > 6 weeks: 4 mg/kg BD (max 200 mg BD) × 3 days (lower UTI) or 7 days (upper).
Co-amoxiclav
Oral — Broader spectrum
25 mg/kg BD (amoxicillin component) × 7–10 days. Good if trimethoprim resistance suspected.
Ceftriaxone IV
IV — Urosepsis / infant < 3 months
50–80 mg/kg IV OD (max 2 g). < 3 months → add ampicillin to cover Listeria/Enterococcus.
Bacterial Meningitis
👶 Paediatrics🚨 EmergencyICD-10: G00
⏱️ Time critical: Do NOT delay antibiotics for LP if signs of sepsis/meningococcaemia. Give IM/IV antibiotics immediately if non-blanching rash. LP can follow when safe.
🔍 Diagnosis
Classic triad: Fever + neck stiffness + photophobia — only 44% of cases. Non-blanching rash = meningococcal until proven otherwise
Infant signs: Bulging fontanelle, high-pitched cry, poor feeding, irritability, opisthotonus
LP CSF: Bacterial: turbid, WBC >1000 (predominantly neutrophils), low glucose, high protein
Causative organisms: Neonates: GBS, E. coli, Listeria. > 1 month: N. meningitidis, S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae
💊 Emergency Treatment
Ceftriaxone IV
Empirical antibiotic — First-line (> 3 months)
80–100 mg/kg IV OD (max 4 g/day). Continue for 7–14 days depending on organism.
Ampicillin + Cefotaxime
Neonatal meningitis (< 3 months)
Ampicillin 50 mg/kg IV q6h + Cefotaxime 50 mg/kg IV q6h. Covers GBS, Listeria, E. coli.
Dexamethasone
Adjunctive — Reduces neurological sequelae
0.15 mg/kg IV q6h × 4 days. Start with or before first dose of antibiotics.
Evidence strongest for S. pneumoniae and H. influenzae meningitis. Not for neonates.
Type 1 Diabetes
👶 PaediatricsICD-10: E10
👶 DKA risk: Any new-onset T1DM child with pH < 7.3, HCO₃ < 15, or ketonemia — admit and treat as DKA. Children deteriorate faster than adults.
🔍 Diagnosis
Classic presentation: Polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss, fatigue. New-onset enuresis in toilet-trained child
Confirm: Fasting glucose > 7 mmol/L or random > 11.1 mmol/L + symptoms. GAD/IA2/ZnT8 antibodies
HbA1c target: < 48 mmol/mol (6.5%) — balance with hypoglycaemia risk and lifestyle
💊 Insulin Therapy
Basal-bolus regimen
Standard management
Total daily dose: 0.5–1 U/kg/day. Split 50% basal (glargine/detemir) + 50% bolus (lispro/aspart) with meals.
Adjust dose by 10–20% based on glucose patterns, not single readings.
CSII (Insulin pump)
Continuous subcutaneous insulin infusion
Consider for: HbA1c > 69 mmol/mol, frequent hypoglycaemia, dawn phenomenon, infants.
Combined with CGM (closed-loop / hybrid closed-loop system) for tightest control.
Febrile Seizure
👶 PaediatricsICD-10: R56.06 months – 5 years
👶 Simple vs Complex: Simple: generalised, < 15 min, single episode in 24h, rapid recovery. Complex: focal, > 15 min, multiple in 24h, or prolonged post-ictal → full septic screen + neurology input.
🔍 Diagnosis
Rule out meningitis: Any stiff neck, purpuric rash, prolonged postictal drowsiness, complex features → LP
Recurrence risk: 30–35% overall. Risk factors: family history, age < 18 months, low-grade fever at onset
Epilepsy risk: 2–4% (vs 1% general population). Higher with complex febrile seizures
💊 Acute Management
Diazepam (rectal) / Midazolam (buccal)
Seizure termination — pre-hospital/ED
Buccal midazolam: 0.3 mg/kg (max 10 mg). Rectal diazepam: 0.5 mg/kg. Give if seizure > 5 min.
Buccal midazolam now preferred over rectal diazepam — more acceptable to parents/carers.
IV Lorazepam / Diazepam
Hospital — If still seizing
Lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV (max 4 mg). Repeat once if needed. Then phenobarbital 20 mg/kg IV.
Antipyretics
Comfort only — NOT preventive
Paracetamol 15 mg/kg PO/PR q4–6h. Ibuprofen 10 mg/kg PO q6–8h.
Antipyretics do NOT prevent recurrent febrile seizures — reassure parents.
STEMI Protocol
🚨 Cardiac EmergencyICD-10: I21Door-to-Balloon < 90 min
🚨 Activate NOW: 12-lead ECG within 10 min of arrival. STEMI criteria → activate cath lab immediately. Call cardiology. Transfer to PCI-capable centre if needed.
⚡ Immediate Actions (0–10 min)
MONAC: Morphine 2.5–5 mg IV (pain) + O₂ only if SpO₂ < 90% + Nitrates (avoid if RV MI or hypotension) + Aspirin 300 mg + Clopidogrel/Ticagrelor loading
IV access × 2: Bloods (FBC, U&E, troponin, clotting, G&S, lipids, HbA1c, glucose)
12-lead ECG: Look for LBBB, posterior MI (V7-V9), RV MI (V3R-V4R)
Echocardiography: Bedside echo if diagnosis uncertain — wall motion abnormalities
💊 Emergency Pharmacotherapy
Aspirin + Ticagrelor
DAPT loading — Give immediately
Aspirin 300 mg PO STAT. Ticagrelor 180 mg PO STAT (or Prasugrel 60 mg if < 75 yrs, > 60 kg, no stroke history).
Unfractionated Heparin (UFH)
Anticoagulation — Pre-PCI
60–70 U/kg IV bolus (max 5000 U) then 12–15 U/kg/hr infusion. Stop post-PCI.
Tenecteplase (TNK)
Fibrinolysis — If PCI not available within 120 min
Weight-based IV bolus (30–50 mg). Administer within 30 min of presentation.
Thrombolyse if: > 120 min to PCI, no contraindications, within 12h of symptom onset.
VF / Pulseless VT
🚨 Cardiac ArrestALS Protocol
🚨 ALS 2021: Every 2-minute CPR cycle. Defibrillate as soon as possible. Adrenaline every 3–5 min (after 3rd shock). Amiodarone after 3rd shock.
⚡ ALS Shockable Rhythm Protocol
Start CPR: 30:2 compressions:ventilations. Rate 100–120/min. Depth 5–6 cm. Rotate every 2 min.
Shock 1: 200 J (biphasic). Resume CPR immediately (2 min) before reassessing rhythm.
Shock 2: 200 J biphasic. After shock 3 → Adrenaline 1 mg + Amiodarone 300 mg IV.
4H/4T reversible causes: Hypoxia, Hypovolaemia, Hyper/hypoK, Hypothermia / Tension pneumo, Tamponade, Toxins, Thrombosis
💊 Cardiac Arrest Drugs
Adrenaline (Epinephrine)
1 mg IV q3–5 min — After 3rd shock
1 mg (10 mL of 1:10,000) IV/IO bolus. Repeat every 3–5 min (every alternate 2-min CPR cycle).
Amiodarone
Antiarrhythmic — After 3rd shock
300 mg IV/IO bolus after 3rd shock. Second dose 150 mg after 5th shock.
Sodium Bicarbonate
Metabolic acidosis / Hyperkalaemia
50 mmol (50 mL 8.4%) IV/IO. Only if pH < 7.1 or hyperkalaemia-induced arrest. Not routine.
Anaphylaxis
🚨 Allergic EmergencyICD-10: T78.2
🚨 ADRENALINE FIRST: Give IM adrenaline immediately on clinical diagnosis. Do NOT wait for investigations. Position supine with legs elevated (or upright if respiratory distress).
⚡ Immediate Management
Recognition: Sudden onset ≥ 2 systems (skin, respiratory, CV, GI) OR severe isolated hypotension/bronchospasm after allergen exposure
Call for help: Crash team. Remove trigger. Call 999 / resus team.
Position: Supine + legs elevated. Sit up if airway compromise. DO NOT stand patient up.
Airway: High-flow O₂ 15 L/min via NRB mask. Early intubation if laryngeal oedema developing.
💊 Drug Protocol
Adrenaline (Epinephrine)
🚨 FIRST-LINE — Give immediately IM
Adult: 0.5 mg IM (0.5 mL of 1:1000) into anterolateral thigh. Child: 0.3 mg (>30 kg) or 0.15 mg (<30 kg). Repeat at 5 min if no improvement.
IM route superior to SC. IV adrenaline ONLY for refractory anaphylaxis in ICU setting.
IV Fluid Resuscitation
Vasodilation / Shock
500–1000 mL 0.9% saline IV bolus over 5–10 min. Repeat as needed for persistent hypotension.
Chlorphenamine + Hydrocortisone
Adjuncts — After adrenaline and fluids
Chlorphenamine 10 mg IV. Hydrocortisone 200 mg IV. Prevent biphasic reaction (not proven but standard practice).
Do NOT delay adrenaline to give antihistamines. These are secondary treatments.
Tension Pneumothorax
🚨 Respiratory EmergencyICD-10: J93.0
🚨 DO NOT WAIT FOR CXR: If clinical diagnosis — immediate needle decompression. Clinical signs: tracheal deviation away, absent breath sounds, hypotension, distended neck veins, hypoxia.
⚡ Immediate Procedure
Needle decompression: 2nd ICS, MCL (anterior approach) OR 4th/5th ICS, AAL (safer). Insert large-bore (14G) cannula.
Definitive treatment: Chest drain — 4th or 5th ICS, AAL. Seldinger or surgical technique.
If ventilated: Very rapid deterioration. Disconnect ventilator — immediate needle decompression.
Post-decompression: CXR to confirm re-expansion. Watch for re-expansion pulmonary oedema.
💊 Supportive Care
High-flow Oxygen
All patients
15 L/min via NRB mask. Accelerates pneumothorax absorption in spontaneous pneumothorax.
IV Analgesia
Pain management
Morphine 2.5–5 mg IV titrated. Or paracetamol 1 g IV + fentanyl 25–50 µg IV if haemodynamically unstable.
Status Epilepticus
🚨 Neuro EmergencyICD-10: G41Seizure > 5 min
⏱️ Time is brain: Treat any seizure > 5 min as status. Each minute of status doubles mortality. Follow the tiered protocol strictly. Call neurology/anaesthesia early.
⚡ Timed Protocol
0–5 min: Position, protect airway, O₂ 15 L/min. IV/IO access × 2. Bloods (glucose, electrolytes, AED levels, toxicology)
5–20 min (Phase 1): Lorazepam 4 mg IV OR midazolam 10 mg buccal/IM. Repeat ONCE if no response at 10 min.
20–40 min (Phase 2): Levetiracetam 60 mg/kg IV (max 4500 mg) OR sodium valproate 40 mg/kg IV (max 3 g) OR phenytoin 20 mg/kg IV.
> 40 min (Refractory): RSI + ICU. Propofol/thiopental/midazolam infusion under EEG monitoring. Neurology consult.
💊 Drug Dosing
Lorazepam IV
First-line benzodiazepine
4 mg IV (adult). Can repeat once at 10 min. Dilute 1:1 with 0.9% saline. Give over 2 min.
Levetiracetam (Keppra)
Second-line AED (preferred — ESETT trial)
60 mg/kg IV over 10 min (max 4500 mg). Preferred over phenytoin — fewer cardiac side effects.
Propofol infusion
Refractory status — RSI/ICU
1–2 mg/kg IV bolus for induction → infusion 1–10 mg/kg/hr. Beware propofol infusion syndrome (>48h).
Acute Stroke
🚨 Neuro EmergencyICD-10: I63Time < 4.5h for thrombolysis
🧠 FAST + BEFAST: Balance, Eyes, Face, Arms, Speech, Time. Activate stroke pathway immediately. CT head within 30 min. Thrombolysis decision within 60 min of arrival.
⚡ Hyperacute Assessment
NIHSS score: Assess on arrival — quantifies deficit severity and guides intervention threshold
Non-contrast CT head: Immediately — to exclude haemorrhage before thrombolysis. Look for early ischaemic changes
CT angiography: Large vessel occlusion (LVO) — if patient is thrombectomy candidate (NIHSS ≥ 6)
Bloods: Glucose (must exclude hypoglycaemia!), FBC, clotting, U&E, ECG for AF
💊 Reperfusion Therapy
Alteplase (rt-PA)
IV Thrombolysis — < 4.5h from onset
0.9 mg/kg IV (max 90 mg): 10% as bolus over 1 min, remaining 90% over 60 min. Monitor BP < 185/110 during infusion.
Absolute contraindications: ICH, recent major surgery, anticoagulation, platelets < 100, glucose < 2.7.
Mechanical Thrombectomy
LVO — Up to 24h with perfusion imaging
Refer to interventional neuroradiology immediately if LVO confirmed on CTA. Can combine with thrombolysis.
Superior to thrombolysis alone for LVO. Target time: < 90 min groin puncture from CT.
Aspirin 300 mg
Antiplatelet — If no thrombolysis
300 mg PO/NGT within 24h of diagnosis (not before thrombolysis). Continue for 2 weeks then reassess.
DKA Protocol
🚨 Metabolic EmergencyICD-10: E10.1
🚨 DKA diagnostic criteria: Glucose > 11 mmol/L + Ketonaemia ≥ 3 mmol/L (or ketonuria ++) + pH < 7.3 (or HCO₃ < 15). Fixed-rate IV insulin infusion (FRIII) at 0.1 U/kg/hr.
⚡ Immediate Management (0–1h)
Severity assessment: Mild: pH 7.25–7.3, HCO₃ 15–18. Moderate: pH 7.0–7.25. Severe: pH < 7.0 (HDU/ITU)
IV fluid resuscitation: 1 L 0.9% saline over 1h (if SBP < 90: 500 mL bolus first). Then 1 L 2-hourly × 2, then 1 L 4-hourly.
Potassium: Check K⁺ BEFORE starting insulin. If K⁺ < 3.5 → replace first. Add 40 mmol KCl/L to fluids if K⁺ 3.5–5.5.
Monitor: Hourly ketones, glucose, GCS. 2-hourly U&E. Target: ketones falling ≥ 0.5 mmol/L/hr
💊 DKA Drug Protocol
Fixed-Rate IV Insulin (FRIII)
Actrapid / Novorapid IV infusion
0.1 U/kg/hr via syringe pump. Do NOT give IV bolus. Continue until ketones < 0.3 mmol/L + pH > 7.3.
Add 10% glucose alongside if glucose < 14 mmol/L (to continue clearing ketones with insulin).
0.9% Sodium Chloride
IV fluid resuscitation
Protocol-based. Typical: 1L/hr × 1h → 1L/2h × 2 → 1L/4h × 2 → 1L/6h × 2. Total ~5–8L in 24h.
VTE Prophylaxis
Low molecular weight heparin
Enoxaparin 40 mg SC OD (if eGFR > 30). DKA is a high-VTE-risk state due to dehydration.
Sepsis & Septic Shock
🚨 Critical EmergencyICD-10: A41
🚨 Hour-1 Bundle (SSC 2021): ① Lactate ② Blood cultures × 2 ③ Broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour ④ 30 mL/kg crystalloid if hypotensive or lactate ≥ 4 ⑤ Vasopressors if MAP < 65
⚡ Diagnostic Criteria
Sepsis-3: Suspected infection + acute SOFA increase ≥ 2. qSOFA: RR ≥ 22, altered mentation, SBP ≤ 100
Septic shock: Vasopressors needed to maintain MAP ≥ 65 + lactate > 2 mmol/L despite fluids → mortality > 40%
Source: Pan-culture + CXR + consider CT. Blood cultures BEFORE antibiotics (but do not delay >45 min)
💊 Antimicrobial & Vasopressors
Pip-Tazo + Gentamicin
Empirical broad-spectrum — Within 1 hour
Pip-Tazo 4.5 g IV TDS. Gentamicin 5 mg/kg IV OD × 1–3 doses (monitor levels). De-escalate at 24–48h.
Norepinephrine (Noradrenaline)
Vasopressor — First choice in septic shock
0.01–3 µg/kg/min via central line. Titrate to MAP ≥ 65. Add vasopressin 0.03 U/min if dose > 0.25 µg/kg/min.
Hydrocortisone
Corticosteroid — Refractory shock
200 mg/day IV (50 mg q6h or continuous infusion). Use if norepinephrine > 0.25 µg/kg/min persisting.
APROCCHSS trial: improved vasopressor-free days and 90-day survival in refractory shock.
